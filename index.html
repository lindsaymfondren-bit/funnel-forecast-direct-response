Model how your funnel converts traffic into leads, customers, and revenue before you spend.

Start with your budget (ad spend) and conversion assumptions to model outcomes. Use this tool to estimate leads, buyers, revenue, ROAS, and break-even targets across common direct-response funnels. It is built for planning, not prediction. Small changes in conversion rate or cost can significantly impact results.

Which calculator should I use?
  • Lead Magnet: estimate lead volume and cost per lead
  • Tripwire: model a low-ticket front-end offer with an optional bump
  • Digital Product: forecast sales-page performance with an optional bump and OTO
  • Webinar: estimate opt-ins, purchases, and break-even for a webinar funnel
  • VSL / Call Booking: model booked calls, show-ups, closes, and revenue
Benchmarks & Limits

Default values are directional starting points, not guarantees. Colder traffic, tighter audiences, weak creative, thin margins, and niche offers can all change the math fast. Use this for scenario planning, then replace defaults with your actual numbers.

How to use it

Keep the inputs in one time frame.

Weekly, monthly, or quarterly all work. Just keep every input in the same time frame so the math stays consistent. Use your own historical numbers whenever you have them.

Lead Magnet
Forecast lead volume and cost per lead from paid traffic. This is useful when the front end is meant to feed the rest of the funnel.
Purpose: Drive paid traffic to a free offer and estimate the volume and cost of leads entering the rest of your funnel.
Benchmarks: Meta-style paid social often lands around 1–3% link click-through rate (CTR), while cost per lead (CPL) can vary widely by audience, niche, and offer. Use your own cost per lead (CPL) when you have it.
Watch out for: Cheap leads are not the same thing as qualified leads. Low cost can still hide poor fit.

Tripwire
Low-ticket front-end offer with an optional bump. Built to create buyers and recover some acquisition cost, not necessarily to carry all the profit alone.
Purpose: Model a low-ticket offer that turns leads into buyers quickly, even if it only breaks even or partially offsets acquisition cost.
Benchmarks: Purchase rates often land around 3–10% of leads. A healthier default for bump purchase rate is 20–40%, not top-performer numbers.
Watch out for: Tripwires are feeder offers. A modest return on ad spend (ROAS) can still be acceptable if the back end monetizes new buyers later.

Sales Page Funnel
Clicks to a sales page for a direct-response offer with optional bump and one-time offer (OTO).
Purpose: Model a direct-response digital offer where paid traffic hits a sales page and optional post-purchase offers lift Average Order Value (AOV).
Benchmarks: Cold paid traffic often lands around 1.5–2% link click-through rate (CTR), 1–5% sales-page conversion, 20–40% bump purchase rate, and 5–15% one-time offer (OTO) purchase rate.
Watch out for: If traffic clicks but the purchase rate is weak, the problem is usually the offer-page experience, not cost per 1,000 impressions (CPM). The main product is usually priced above the bump order.

Webinar
Opt-ins to a live or automated webinar with one blended purchase rate.
Purpose: Estimate webinar economics using one blended opt-in-to-purchase rate instead of modeling registration, attendance, and offer conversion separately.
Benchmarks: Webinar cost per lead (CPL) can vary widely, but 3–10% overall opt-in-to-purchase is a more realistic starting range than top-performer numbers unless you know your audience is warm.
Watch out for: This is a shortcut model. A weak show-up rate or a weak pitch can hide inside that one conversion field, so adjust with care.

VSL / Call Booking
Opt-ins watch a VSL, book a call, show up, and close.
Purpose: Forecast a Video Sales Letter (VSL) or call-booking funnel where paid traffic turns into leads, booked calls, attended calls, and closed sales.
Benchmarks: Book rates often land around 5–20%, show-up rates around 50–65%, and close rates around 20–30% are a safer starting point than top-performer numbers.
Watch out for: Small improvements at each stage compound. This model is useful for planning, but real-world sales behavior will always vary.
